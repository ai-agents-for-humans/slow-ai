## Design Direction

**Tone:** Thoughtful. Confident without being loud. Anti-hype.
The opposite of every breathless AI launch page.

**Visual language:** Dark background. Slow, deliberate motion.
Graphs that render node by node. Text that doesn't rush.
White space used generously — because space is where thinking happens.

**Typography:** A serious serif for headlines. Clean monospace for
anything technical. Nothing rounded or playful.

**Palette:** Deep navy / near-black background. Amber or warm gold
as the accent — deliberate, not urgent. White text. Muted greys
for secondary content.

**No**: countdown timers, waitlists, "🚀 launching soon", follower counts,
logos of companies using it, fake social proof, auto-playing video.

**Yes**: one real story told honestly, clear diagrams, the actual GitHub,
a manifesto that means something.

## Section 2 — The Problem

### Section label
`The gap nobody is talking about`

### Headline

**We are operating heavy machinery
without reading the instruments.**

### Body

There is a wave right now. Everyone is building with agents. Automating
workflows. Shipping AI features. Moving fast.

And almost nobody can tell you what their agents actually did to produce
that output. What they searched. What they concluded. Where they got
stuck. What they chose to ignore.

We handed people the keys to an excavator and told them to trust the bucket.

This is not a technology problem. It is a design problem.
The tools we have were built for speed. They were not built for understanding.
And a system that cannot be understood cannot be improved.

The result: knowledge workers — analysts, researchers, strategists,
scientists, consultants — are being handed AI outputs they cannot
interrogate, cannot verify, and cannot learn from.

That is not augmentation. That is a different kind of dependency.

### Pull quote

> "Fast is the wrong KPI to chase. Or rather — the *only* KPI to chase.
> We need to be able to do a lot more with agents than just fast."

## Section 3 — The Learning Argument

### Section label
`On getting better`

### Headline

**Agents need to learn.
Context needs to be recorded.
Systems need to improve.
Why should yours be any different?**

### Body

Every expert gets better through a cycle:
do the work, review the record, close the gaps, repeat.

The surgeon reviews the case notes.
The analyst goes back to the model that was wrong.
The scientist reads what the last experiment couldn't determine.

This is how expertise compounds. Not through speed.
Through deliberate review of what happened and why.

Slow AI is built around this loop.

Every run produces a complete record — not a chat log, but a structured
audit of everything that happened. Every phase synthesised. Every
agent's confidence scored. Every gap catalogued.

The skills the system couldn't execute become a backlog.
The next run builds on what the last run found.
The brief gets sharper because the interview taught you something.
The context graph improves because you've run this class of problem before.

Over time the system doesn't just run your investigations.
It gets better at running them.

That is the difference between a tool you use and a system you build.

**Gaps compound into capability**

## Section 4 — No More Drawing Boxes

### Section label
`For teams and enterprises`

### Headline

**Every other workflow tool asks you
to design the workflow first.**

### Body

Draw the DAG. Connect the nodes. Map the failure paths.
Define what happens when a step breaks.
Find someone technical enough to maintain it.

n8n. Zapier. LangGraph. Every enterprise agentic framework.
They are all, at their core, asking the same question:
*Can you describe your process as a flowchart?*

Most knowledge workers cannot. And they shouldn't have to.

Slow AI starts with the problem, not the pipeline.

Describe what you need to understand in plain language.
The interview clarifies it. The system designs the workflow.
A context graph emerges — phases, parallel investigations,
the shape of the problem made visible.

Then your team runs it. This week, next quarter, across every
client, every market, every dataset. The workflow is reusable
because it is problem-shaped, not tool-shaped.

No DAG. No maintenance. No engineer required to change a step.
The knowledge worker who knows the domain runs the domain investigation.

## Section 5 — The Story

### Section label
`A real investigation`

### Headline

**A knee injury. A research question.
A team of agents that went to work.**

### Body

My knee has been injured for years. A cartilage problem that needs surgery —
surgery I haven't been able to have.

So I did what any person does: I searched. I read. I got overwhelmed
by medical papers, clinic websites, forum posts, and conflicting advice.
I couldn't tell efficacy from marketing.

Then I used Slow AI to investigate it properly.

First: an interview. Not a search box — a conversation. The system
asked me questions I hadn't thought to ask myself. It pushed back on
vague assumptions. By the end of the interview I understood my own
problem more clearly than I had before I started.

Then it built a context graph. A structured breakdown of the research
question — non-invasive procedures available locally, clinical efficacy
data, patient outcome studies, cost and accessibility. The shape of the
problem, made visible.

Then the agents went to work.

Some searched medical databases. Some downloaded and parsed clinical
papers. Some wrote code to cross-reference datasets. Some called APIs
to check what was available in my city. Each agent did the kind of work
that kind of question actually requires.

And I could see all of it.

Every phase. Every finding. Every confidence score.
Every place an agent got stuck and said *I couldn't find enough evidence*.

At the end I had something no search engine had given me: not just
information, but *understanding*. I knew what had been looked at,
what hadn't, what the agents were confident about, and what remained
genuinely uncertain.

That uncertainty — honestly reported — was itself valuable.
It told me what questions to bring to a doctor.

### Pull quote

> "At the end I didn't just have an answer.
> I had a collaborator. And I knew exactly how we got there."

## Section 6 — How It Works

### Section label
`The experience`

### Headline

**Five acts. One continuous thread of understanding.**

*Use this section with screen recordings of each act.*

---

### Act I — The Interview

**Your problem, clarified.**

Before any agent runs, Slow AI interviews you.
One question at a time. It pushes back on vagueness.
It surfaces assumptions you didn't know you were making.

By the time you confirm your brief, you understand
your own problem better than when you started.
That is not a side effect. It is the point.

*[Screen recording: conversational interview UI, question by question]*

---

### Act II — The Context Graph

**The shape of your problem, made visible.**

Your brief becomes a context graph — a structured decomposition
of the work into phases and parallel investigations.

This is how expert thinking actually works: breaking a hard problem
into its component parts, understanding which depend on which,
identifying what needs to be true before the next question can be asked.

You review this graph before anything runs.
You can change it. Refine it. Tell the system what you want to add
or remove. The graph is yours.

*[Screen recording: graph rendering, chat refinement, launch]*

---

### Act III — The Agent Swarm

**Agents that do the work the work actually requires.**

When you launch, a swarm of specialist agents goes to work in parallel —
each assigned to one piece of the graph, each equipped with only the
tools that piece of work actually needs.

Some search. Some browse. Some write and execute code.
Some download and parse datasets. Some call APIs.
Some discover they need a capability that doesn't exist yet —
and the system synthesizes it from the tools it has.

No single agent does everything.
Each does one thing well.

*[Screen recording: live agent DAG growing in real time]*

---

### Act IV — Transparency

**See everything. Not just the answer.**

This is the part that changes how you think about AI.

Every agent produces an evidence envelope: what it found, how confident
it is, what it couldn't determine, what it tried and abandoned.

Every phase produces a synthesis: what the agents collectively established,
where they contradicted each other, what the next phase needs to know.

You see confidence scores. You see what was covered and what was partial.
You see where the system hit a wall and why.

The output is not a result. It is a record.

*[Screen recording: context graph coverage overlay, clicking into an envelope]*

---

### Act V — Conversation and Continuation

**Ask anything. Get answers with receipts.
Then go further.**

When the run completes, the system generates a briefing —
a structured narrative of what was found, phase by phase,
with citations linked to the specific agents and evidence that produced them.

Then you can talk to it. Ask follow-up questions.
Drill into a specific agent's reasoning.
Request the actual dataset it downloaded.
Understand why confidence was low on a particular question.

And if there is more to investigate: continue the run.
Build on what was already found. Don't repeat covered ground.
The next run starts with the memory of every run before it.

## Section 8 — The Philosophy

### Section label
`Three things we believe`

---

### Bring Your Own Models

You should not have to trust a platform's model choices.
Slow AI routes each task type to whichever model you configure —
Google, Anthropic, OpenAI, or a local model running on your own hardware.

Change a line in a JSON file. No code. No rebuilds.
Your workflows. Your models. Your infrastructure.

---

### Sovereignty Over Your Workflows

When you run an investigation with Slow AI, the context graph,
the agent plans, the evidence, and the final report all live on your disk.
In a git repository. Committed with full history.

You are not uploading your business processes to a platform.
You are not training someone else's model on your proprietary questions.
You own the system of record.

---

### Knowledge Workers Are Not Replaceable

The agent swarm does not replace the expert.
It works for the expert.

The analyst who knows their domain brings the questions.
The system brings the scale to investigate them properly.

What you get at the end is not AI output you have to take on faith.
It is evidence you can interrogate, reasoning you can follow,
and gaps you can decide to close — or accept.

That is augmentation. The other thing has a different name.

Slow AI is an open-source system for running multi-agent research workflows

- You need to understand *how* a conclusion was reached, not just what it is
- You want your workflows, evidence, and findings on your own infrastructure
- You want a system that learns from every run, not one that forgets

## Notes for Claude Design

**Key visual moments to design around:**

1. **The learning loop** — a circular or spiral diagram showing:
   Problem → Interview → Context Graph → Agent Swarm → Record →
   Review → Sharper Brief → next run. Each loop tighter than the last.
   This is the hero visual. Slow, deliberate animation.

2. **The context graph** — phases as larger nodes, work items as smaller
   nodes within each phase, dependency edges between phases. Feels like
   a mind map drawn by a careful thinker, not a flowchart.
   Render it progressively — nodes appear one by one.

3. **The live agent DAG** — a tree of agents appearing in real time.
   Each node colour-coded: grey (waiting), blue (running), green
   (complete), red (failed). Animate the edges drawing between nodes.

4. **The coverage overlay** — the same context graph after the run,
   now showing green (covered), amber (partial), grey (not reached).
   The work, made visible. One of the most important images on the site.

5. **The evidence envelope** — a card: role, confidence score as a gauge,
   sources listed, what it couldn't determine called out explicitly.
   Honest and legible.

6. **The chained runs diagram** — three runs shown as a timeline,
   each one building on the last. Prior evidence flows forward.
   Skill gaps close. The brief sharpens. Shows the compounding effect.

**Screen recordings needed:**
- The interview: conversational brief elicitation, question by question
- Graph review: graph rendering, chat refinement, launch button click
- Live run: agent DAG growing in real time, phase summary cards appearing
- Post-run: the run summary briefing, drilling into an agent envelope,
  asking a follow-up question
- Continuation: "do what we didn't finish" → follow-on graph review,
  showing agents skip already-covered ground

**Key phrases for visual callouts:**
- "Systems that learn. Agents that work. Humans who stay in control."
- "Every decision, visible"
- "The work, not just the answer"
- "Your models. Your infrastructure. Your record."
- "Honest AI shows you where it got stuck"
- "No DAG. No pipeline. Just describe the problem."
